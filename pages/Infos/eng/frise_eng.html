JayavarmanII

Date : 11th century-12nd century

Considered one of the greatest kings of ancient Cambodia, he is the founder of Angkorian power. In 802, he claimed Khmer independence from the Javanese and established the cult of devarāja (Hindu rites) as the official religion. He established a series of capitals, first in Indrapura, on the lower Mekong River east of Kâmpóng (Kompong) Cham; then, moving northward, in Hariharalaya, southeast of the current Siem Reap; and then to Mahendraparvata (now Roluos), in the region north of the Tonle Sap (Great Lake), about ten kilometres from the current Angkor, the next seat of the Khmer empire, which remained its capital for 600 years. His reign allowed the unification of the Khmer kingdom then divided into different provinces

Indravarman 1

Indravarman I succeeds Jayavarman III and is at the origin of a great expansion of his empire in a peaceful way. Its main achievement was the construction of a reservoir that is now dry. In 879, he had the Preah Ko temple built.

X

Yasovarman I

Yasovarman I, son and successor of Indravarman, was the first to settle on the Angkor site. There are at least a hundred monasteries built throughout his kingdom, which served as a resting place for the king. In 893, he built the Baray Oriental, a reserve in the middle of a lake. In addition, he had two temples built: Phnom Bok and Phnom Krom

Suryavarman I

Suryavarman I (សូរ្យវរ្ម្ម័នទី១; posthumously Nirvanapada), is known as a conquering king since he extended his territorial possessions and consolidated the conquered lands into a strong and unified empire. He was a strong and competent leader who knew prayer, rituals, sacrifices and astronomy, his reign was marked by peace and prosperity, indeed no revolt was made.

Jayavarman VII

Jayavarman VII spent all his youth fighting the Chams and was crowned in 1181 at the age of 50. He is considered one of the last great Khmer kings because he was able to recover in a very short time a country that was in the process of material, cultural and moral loss. It was under the influence of his second wife, Princess Indradevi, that Jayavarman II replaced the devaraja cult by that of Buddha-roi and worshipped the bodhisatva Lokesvara.He had 102 hospitals, cottages, roads and bridges built, and schools for monks. Nicknamed the "Builder King", he is the one who built the most temples in Angkor. He is responsible for his major work: The Bayon but also: the ramparts, the five gates and the renovation of Angkor Thom, the terraces known as the Elephants and the Leper King, the Prah Khan, a vast monastery dedicated to his father, the Ta Prohm in memory of his mother, the Neak Pean, the Bantéa Kdeï but also the Ta Prohm, the Banteay Chhmar and the Vat Nokor; Jayavarman VII gave his heart to his people: "The suffering of the people is his". Its main objective was to transform Cambodia into a paradise where the Khmer people would be the Tevada.

Jayavarman VIII

Jayavarman VIII took power from 1243 to 1295. He imposed a return to Hinduism and the cult of the devaraja sovereign (God-king) and finally put an end to the religious tolerance established by his predecessor Jayavarman VII. He will devote himself to converting or destroying all Buddhist temples into Hindu temples and destroying thousands of Buddha statues, some of which are nearly 4 meters high in the Bayon.
